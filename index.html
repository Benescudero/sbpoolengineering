Heat speeds up chemistry, salt cells raise pH, and together they create scale — a major cause of early salt cell failure.

This Isn't Random Failure — It's Environmental Stress

Heat
  • Accelerates chemical reactions
  • Increases stress on coatings and electronics
Elevated pH

What is elevated pH? pH measures how acidic or basic your pool water is. Elevated pH means the water is more basic (alkaline). Salt cells naturally raise pH during chlorine production, which can cause problems.

  • A natural byproduct of salt chlorine generation
  • Encourages mineral precipitation
Scale

What is scale? Scale is a hard, chalky buildup of minerals (like calcium) that forms on surfaces. In salt cells, it builds up on the internal parts and blocks them from working properly.

  • Forms faster under heat and elevated pH
  • Hard water makes scale formation far more aggressive
  • Covers the salt cell's diodes and reduces chlorine productivity

Question
Why can salt cells fail early even with balanced water?
Answer
Salt cells can fail early because electrolysis creates high pH at the diodes, and when water flow stops during off cycles, heat and UV exposure continue — raising temperatures and accelerating scale formation that eventually kills the cell. Salt cells don't usually fail because pool water is "bad." Even properly balanced pools experience scaling because electrolysis naturally raises pH right at the cell's diodes. High pH causes minerals to fall out of solution and stick to the diodes. Heat makes this worse, and hard water accelerates it even faster. During off cycles, water flow stops but heat and sun exposure continue, allowing scale to build unchecked. Over time, this buildup blocks chlorine productivity and leads to premature cell failure.
